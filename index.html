03 / SWOT

The Structural Picture

The SWOT below is structural. It describes the way the firm currently sources, screens, underwrites, and capitalizes deals, not the people executing those activities. The picture it paints is the one that informs the strategic decisions later in the document.

Strengths

  • Twenty-plus years of experience and a proven model that has worked for Fund V investors
  • Decision-making agency. Equity is fully discretionary, which means the principals control deployment
  • Deep market knowledge and an existing relationship infrastructure in greater Houston
  • A low-fee posture that is genuinely attractive to LPs
  • An in-house tenant rep team that produces a constant flow of market data and tenant signal

Weaknesses

  • The current GP-friendly waterfall has worked well for Fund V's friends-and-family base. It is not market for institutional or scale-grade LPs and would likely need to evolve to support raising at the level the three-year goal requires
  • The "value add and opportunistic" mandate is not consistently operationalized when it requires committing on the firm's own conviction rather than tenant rep validation
  • The firm has historically passed on speculative deals based on a small sample of tenant feedback. The internal model for how that feedback gets weighed against the principals' own real estate conviction is not made explicit, which limits delegation
  • The weekly screening cadence is informal and conversational, not template-driven on the front end
  • Templates exist but are concentrated on the under-contract and PSA stage. Front-end screening templates are limited
  • Underwriting is gated on IRR even though the average hold runs five to ten years and IRR is largely an exit-driven metric
  • Investment criteria are written broadly enough that the firm's specific entrepreneurial posture is harder to communicate to brokers and prospective partners

Opportunities

  • Process redesign on the front end of deal screening, including a sell-hold analysis on every deal as part of the underwriting standard
  • A centralized CRM that combines relationships, deals, equity opportunities, and development pipeline in one place
  • A market-data rolodex that captures asks and signed rents, active deals, and tenant movement so the data the brokerage team is generating becomes accessible across the firm
  • AI tooling for the upfront work: lease abstracts, title document review, screening triage, and other administrative load that currently sits in human hours
  • Refining or rebuilding the internal Excel models so they align with the actual hold-and-operate profile of the existing portfolio
  • Targeted growth in the Top-20 list categories that are currently underdeveloped: lenders and capital, master-planned community developers, and land owners and brokers

Threats

  • Institutional capital flooding back into Texas retail. Fee-driven competitors charging two to five points on acquisition, plus annual asset management fees, plus disposition fees, against Baker Katz's promote-only economics
  • A post-COVID shift in the broker and owner ecosystem. Smaller shops, several of them with younger people in regular contact with our acquisitions team, are increasingly trying to deploy capital themselves rather than feeding deals to traditional buyers. Specific names showing up consistently: BCS, Palo Duro, DuWest, Wellspring, Jolink Wallace, and Ironbridge
  • The current capital structure makes raising at scale meaningfully harder. The friends-and-family base is loyal but limited in size, and the existing waterfall is unlikely to attract the kinds of investors who can fill the additional ~$55M of equity the three-year goal requires

07 / Decisions Required

The Strategic Choice Points

These are the load-bearing decisions for the next phase. Each one shapes the acquisition strategy in a meaningfully different direction depending on which way it goes. The first one is the most consequential because everything else in the firm's growth story flexes around it.

01

Capital Structure: Status Quo or Scale

The current Fund V structure has worked well for the existing friends-and-family LP base. The waterfall is GP-friendly in a way that has been right for that group of investors. To raise the additional roughly $55M of equity needed to reach the three-year goal, the math says we likely need to attract investors outside that base, and the structure that worked for Fund V is unlikely to work for them. This is the lever that determines whether the three-year goal is achievable through the existing fund structure or whether a meaningfully different capital approach has to come into the picture.

Path A: Hold the current waterfall and accept that the three-year goal flexes to the size of capital the existing base will fund Path B: Move toward a more market-standard waterfall (and likely a new vehicle) to unlock the institutional and scale-grade equity the three-year goal requires
02

Underwriting Gate: IRR or Cash-on-Cash

The portfolio's actual hold profile runs five to ten years. Gating decisions on IRR up front means gating on an exit assumption that, in many cases, is not the binding constraint on whether a deal works. The cash-on-cash threshold that aligns with the firm's actual operating profile is roughly 8% minimum over the same period, with the waterfall pref accrual factored in.

Path A: Keep IRR as the front-end gate Path B: Switch to cash-on-cash as the primary front-end gate with IRR measured on the back end Path C: Use both, with cash-on-cash as the primary gate and IRR as a secondary check
03

Decision Transparency on Tenant Feedback

The "value add and opportunistic" posture is interpreted differently across the team because the model for how tenant rep feedback gets weighed against the principals' own real estate conviction is not explicit. Making it explicit enables the acquisitions team to screen deals more in line with how the two of you actually think. Keeping it intuitive preserves principal judgment but limits how much can be delegated.

Path A: Keep the model intuitive and principal-led Path B: Articulate the model explicitly enough that the acquisitions team can run a first pass on the same logic the two of you would apply
04

Sell/Hold Cadence

The current sell/hold review is ad hoc and not visible beyond the two of you. A rolling cadence makes the firm's track record on returned capital legible in a way that matters when the conversation turns to attracting new LPs.

Path A: Keep the current ad hoc approach Path B: Institute a rolling sell/hold review on a defined cadence with the acquisitions team in the loop
05

Top-20 List: Formal or Informal

The relationship-tracking goal is clearly worth pursuing. The question is the level of formality. A formal CRM-backed list with weekly outreach and monthly check-ins generates more disciplined cultivation but adds operational overhead. An informal version captures less but moves with how relationships actually develop. The current state is somewhere in between and hasn't moved much since January.

Path A: Invest in formalizing (CRM, weekly cadence, named owner) Path B: Keep it informal and let the relationships drive the cadence Path C: Split the difference: formal on a small set of priority categories, informal elsewhere
06

Tech Spend Posture

Tech historically gets added when there is a clear, named use case driving it (the addition of Argus is the recent example). A standing budget with a named owner enables strategic investment. The case-by-case approach protects against waste but tends to lag the curve.

Path A: Continue case-by-case spend driven by use case Path B: Establish a standing tech budget with a named decision-owner
07

CRM and Data Infrastructure

The current substitute (a Google Sheet plus Teams Planner) works for keeping the active and semi-active deal list. It does not solve for relationships, market data, or institutional memory. The choice is what to invest in, given whatever the answer to Decision 06 is.

Path A: Stay with the current setup Path B: Build something custom around the existing tools Path C: Invest in a proper CRM with relationships, deals, equity opportunities, and market-data fields
