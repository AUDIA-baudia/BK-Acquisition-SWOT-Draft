01 / Context

What This Document Is

On Monday, April 27 at 3:00 PM CDT, Brian and Jack ran a 57-minute working session over Google Meet. The format was a SPIN sequence anchored on a single goal: get business development on track and pointed in the right direction, with an acquisition strategy that everyone agrees on from a north star down to the initial steps in how it gets executed. Jack walked through a full SWOT he had prepared from his January planning notes and his eighteen months in seat, and surfaced a set of structural problems that the work ahead will need to solve.

This document does two things. First, it captures what Jack said in a clean, durable format so the working session does not live only inside a Google Meet transcript. Second, it lists the specific questions designed to confirm we have the current state right before we commit to a strategy and the project plan that follows from it. Confirming current state matters because everything downstream rests on this baseline being accurate.

03 / Jack's SWOT

The Picture from the Acquisitions Seat

Jack walked through his SWOT in his own words. The framing below preserves his categories and his examples. The SWOT is structural: it is about the way the firm currently sources, screens, underwrites, and capitalizes deals. None of it is a critique of the people. Jack was clear and consistent on that point.

Strengths

  • Twenty-plus years of experience and a proven model that has worked for investors
  • Decision-making agency. The equity is fully discretionary, which means the principals control deployment
  • Deep market knowledge and an existing relationship infrastructure in greater Houston
  • A GP-friendly waterfall and a low-fee posture, which is unusually attractive to investors

Weaknesses

  • An institutional bias toward downside and worst-case framing despite a stated value-add and opportunistic mandate
  • Deal screening that leans heavily on feedback from one or two represented tenants. One tenant saying no can kill a deal that another tenant might still want
  • A weekly screening cadence that is informal and conversational, not template-driven on the front end
  • Templates exist but are not always intuitive or sufficient for every deal
  • Underwriting gated on IRR, even though the average hold is seven to ten years and IRR is largely an exit-driven metric
  • Investment criteria written broadly enough that "value add and opportunistic" is not consistently operationalized

Opportunities

  • Process redesign on the front end of deal screening and underwriting, including a sell-hold analysis on every deal
  • A centralized CRM that combines relationships, deals, equity opportunities, and development pipeline in one place
  • A market-data rolodex that captures what asks and signed rents look like, which deals are active, and which tenants are moving
  • AI tooling for the upfront work: lease abstracts, title document review, screening triage, and other administrative load
  • Refining or rebuilding internal Excel models so they align with how the firm actually holds and operates assets

Threats

  • Institutional capital flooding back into Texas retail. Fee-driven competitors charging two to five points on acquisition, plus annual asset management fees, plus disposition fees
  • A post-COVID shift in the broker and owner ecosystem. Shops that used to feed deals to Baker Katz are now trying to deploy capital themselves and are no longer first-call partners
  • Constraints on raising new capital. The current GP-friendly structure is excellent for the principals and existing investors but harder to sell to new LPs without a clearer disposition cadence and demonstrated returned capital
04 / The Five Surfaced Problems

Where the 90-Day Sprint Goes to Work

The SWOT collapses to five concrete problem areas. Each one is a candidate workstream for the BD plan. The order below is the order Jack surfaced them, not yet the priority order. Stack-ranking is one of the things we will do together once the current state is confirmed.

01

Deal-Screening SOP

Tighten the front end so the firm stops chasing deals for weeks before walking away.

02

IRR vs Cash-on-Cash

Reconsider which metric gates the front end given the seven-to-ten-year hold profile.

03

Top-20 List

Stand up a working broker, owner, and equity-partner list that survives Monday meetings.

04

Centralized CRM

One place for relationships, deals, equity opportunities, development, and market data.

05

Disposition Cadence

Rolling sell-hold analysis to make the firm investable to new LPs through visible returned capital.

Jack also self-rated the firm's data-centricity at four to five out of ten. Underwriting assumptions and market intel come from the principals' intrinsic knowledge rather than from a queryable system. He named this as a constraint on his own ability to participate in the deal-screening conversation on equal footing. We will treat this as a sixth, cross-cutting condition rather than a standalone problem because it shows up inside every one of the five above.

05 / Current-State Confirmation Questions

What We Need to Confirm Before We Plan

The questions below are organized by the same six themes the SWOT and problem list surfaced. Each one is designed to either validate the current understanding or expose a gap we need to fix before drafting the plan. Take them in order. Where you have an answer, put it down. Where it needs Jason and Kenneth to weigh in, flag it as such.

A

Capital and Fund Structure

Confirming the math behind the gap is the first job. Numbers Jack stated on the call need to be locked in writing.

  • Is $32.5M the precise raise amount, and is "approximately half deployed" the right characterization as of today?
  • What is the exact current expiration date on the fund and what triggered the eighteen-month extension?
  • What does the LP base actually look like today: how many investors, friends-and-family share versus institutional, average commitment size?
  • Is there a written investment period restriction, or only the fund expiration, that limits when new acquisitions can be made?
  • If the goal is roughly $70M of equity over three years, what portion of that is contemplated to come from the existing fund versus a new vehicle, a coinvestment structure, or programmatic equity?
B

Deal-Screening Process

The weekly walk-through deserves a clean current-state map before we propose anything different.

  • Walk through a typical Monday meeting. Who is in the room, who runs the agenda, and how does a deal go from "Jack mentioned it" to "we are pursuing it"?
  • Where do new deal leads land first today: email, broker call, a shared inbox, a spreadsheet, somewhere else?
  • What templates currently exist on the front end, and which ones does Jack feel are not fit for purpose?
  • Can Jack name two or three recent deals where the firm chased it for weeks and then walked away, and what tipped each one?
  • What would a "no" at the front end look like that would feel decisive instead of feeling like a moving target?
C

Underwriting and Models

Argus plus internal Excel is the current stack. Need to ground the IRR-versus-cash-on-cash conversation in actual models.

  • Confirm the current modeling stack: Argus Enterprise plus the two or three internal Excel models. Which model is the actual gating model in the room?
  • What is the documented hold-period assumption used in those models today, and how often does the assumption match the realized hold?
  • If we shifted the front-end gate from IRR to cash-on-cash, what threshold would Jack want to test, and would Jason and Kenneth be open to that change?
  • Is sensitivity analysis (rent growth, cap rate, vacancy, cap-ex) part of the standard underwriting today, or done case by case?
  • What does the sell-hold analysis look like today on existing assets, and how often is it run?
D

Brokerage and Sourcing Ecosystem

The "shops doing it themselves" thesis needs evidence the four-way working session can defend.

  • Which specific shops or individuals has Jack been getting the "we are buying for ourselves first" feedback from, and over what time window?
  • Roughly how many post-COVID shops fit that profile, and how does Jack want to quantify it for the four-way working session?
  • Where does the working top-20 list live today, and what fields does it have on each entry?
  • What does Jack want the top-20 list to do, mechanically: weekly outreach cadence, monthly check-ins, a shared dashboard, a CRM record?
  • Beyond brokers, are there owner-operators, family offices, or developers who should be on a parallel list as equity or co-GP partners?
E

Technology and Data Infrastructure

This is the substance of the May 1 deep-dive. Need a real inventory before recommending anything.

  • What systems are in use today: accounting, asset management, lease administration, CRM, market data (CoStar, Reonomy, Placer, Yardi, MRI, others)?
  • Which of those systems do Jack and the brokerage team actually log into on a weekly basis?
  • What has Jack's Claude experimentation looked like over the past month, and which tasks did it help with most?
  • What does Jack believe the firm's appetite is, in dollar terms, for new technology spend over the next twelve months?
  • Is there an internal owner today for technology decisions, or has it historically been a principal-by-principal call?
F

Disposition and LP Attraction

The capital-raise question depends on demonstrating returned capital. Confirming the disposition picture is part of the baseline.

  • Over the last five years, how many assets has the firm sold, and what is the rough realized IRR distribution on those exits?
  • How is the sell-hold conversation structured today: ad hoc, annual, asset-by-asset trigger?
  • If a new LP asked tomorrow "show me the last three full-cycle deals," what is the answer, and is that answer compelling?
  • What posture do Jason and Kenneth have on programmatic dispositions: open to it, cautious, opposed?
  • Would the principals consider a separate vehicle structured around a shorter hold and faster recycle to attract a different LP profile?
